Plan Your Run

  1. First things first, you need to find a local trail. I enjoy running the firetrails around Liberty Reservoir west of Baltimore, MD. Alltrails.com is a great source to find trails like Liberty and reviews of their condition.
  2. Make sure you dress for where youâ€™re going to run. Trails can be a varied terrain that includes mud and rock. Given the extend time of trail running it's not unusual for rain to pop-up on a run. Have a light running jacket handy if there's a chance of rain.
  3. Make plans in terms of time on the trail and not distance run. Trail running can take a lot more time and covers less distance when compared to running on the road. An 8-minute mile on pavement is easily more than 16 minutes on a trail.
  4. Learn to know where you are. Trail running unlike a track or road doesn't always present an easy sense of where you. Trails that split and overlap can quickly lead to losing your way. A compass is a boon in this situation. Even better is a GPS to help when the path becomes unclear. Alltrails has an app for download with a decent trail map and GPS.
  5. This might be an overlap of number 2 but it's important. Get a good good pair of shoes made for trail running.

The Right Shoe

A pair of normal running shoes won't work on a trail. Trails can be muddy, sandy, rocky, and have extreme shifts in verticality. A normal pair of running shoes will lead to disappoint at best and injury at worst.

Trail running shoes have a more aggressive tread or lugs on the bottom of the shoe to provide grip. This goes from mild on some shoes to almost a cleat like bottom on the most aggressive. You really need this when running on unpredictable paths. Some inclines that would otherwise unpassable become doable with the right tread.

Trail running shoes have something called a Rock Plate. This is a midsole shank that protects your foot from bruising on impact with a rock edge mid stride. This is an absolute must if the trail you're running has rocky spots. The fire trails I run at Liberty Reservoir have a lot of rocky patches and I can tell the difference between wearing shoes with and without some form of rock protection.

My preferred trail running shoes are from Inov-8. Inov-8 has a great selection of shoes for hitting the outdoors. My two favorites are the ROCLITE and TRAILTALON.
